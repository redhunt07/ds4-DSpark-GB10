The afternoon light came through the western windows of the workshop in long, slanting bars that turned the dust hanging in the air into a slow-moving constellation. Esther stood at her bench with her sleeves rolled to the elbow and a small brass file in her hand, working at the inside curve of a clockwork wheel she had been shaping since dawn. Outside the open door, the harbor wind brought the smell of tar and seaweed and the distant cries of gulls quarreling over fish heads on the wharf. She paused, turned the wheel a quarter turn against the morning's light, examined the bevel, and went on with the file. The cat asleep on the high shelf opened one eye, considered her, and closed it again. From the front of the shop she could hear her father's voice in conversation with a customer, low and patient, with the cadence he reserved for those who came in carrying something precious and broken.
