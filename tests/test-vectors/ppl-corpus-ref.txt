The river kept its own counsel. Each morning the surveyor walked the eastern bank
with a brass level and a notebook bound in oilcloth, marking the slow migration of
the channel against the iron pins his predecessor had driven a generation earlier.
The pins were rusted now, and two had vanished entirely beneath a gravel bar that
had not existed the previous spring. He recorded what he could measure and left the
rest to the patience of sediment, which answers every question eventually, though
never on the schedule of the men who ask it.

By noon the heat lay flat across the floodplain. Cattle stood in the shade of the
cottonwoods without moving, and the only sound was the dry tick of grasshoppers in
the fennel. The surveyor ate bread and a hard yellow cheese, drank from a canteen
that tasted faintly of tin, and considered the problem of the meander. A river that
bends will cut its outer bank and build its inner one, and so the bend grows sharper
until the neck pinches shut and the water abandons the loop altogether, leaving a
crescent lake to silt up and become, in time, a meadow.

In the afternoon he climbed the bluff to triangulate from the old benchmark. From
the height the whole valley resolved into a single argument about gradient and load:
where the slope steepened the water ran clear and fast over cobbles; where it slackened
the channel braided and dropped its burden of silt; and at every transition the form
of the bed encoded the history of every flood that had ever passed.

He finished the traverse before the light failed and walked back along the levee. A
heron lifted from the shallows ahead of him, unfolding with the deliberate economy of
something that has never once in its life been in a hurry, and beat slowly downstream
until the dusk took it. The pins would still be there in the morning, or some of them
would, and the channel would have moved an imperceptible increment closer to the town,
and the calculation would begin again.
